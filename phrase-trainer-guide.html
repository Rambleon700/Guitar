Phrase Trainer — Complete Guide

How to use the
Phrase Trainer

The Phrase Trainer gives you a looping metronome, a live chord display, and a fretboard that shows exactly where to land. This guide walks through every control, what it does, and how to sequence your practice from day one to fluent phrasing.

The idea

One simple rule

Most scale practice trains your fingers to travel through notes. Music is about landing on notes. The difference is targeting.

The trainer gives you a chord and a beat. Your job is to end phrases on a note that belongs to that chord — ideally the root — on beat 1. That single habit, applied consistently, is what makes improvisation sound musical instead of like a scale exercise.

Everything else in the tool exists to make that one habit easier to build.

Key sets the tonic — the home note everything orbits. Start in A. It sits naturally in the lower fret positions and is the most common key for backing tracks you'll find online.

Mode switches between Minor and Major pentatonic. Minor is the blues/rock default. Major has a brighter, country feel. The fretboard and chord tones update immediately when you switch.

Prog chooses the chord progression the trainer loops through. Each chord gets one bar. See the full progression reference below.

BPM is the tempo. Drag the slider left to slow down, right to speed up. The amber number shows the current value. Start at 60–70. Slower than feels comfortable. Speed only after landing becomes automatic.

Show controls which dots appear on the fretboard. This is the most important practice lever — see the full explanation below.

Transport

Play button and beat dots

The amber circle starts and stops the metronome. Press once to start; press again to stop. The four dots to the right show you where you are in the bar.

Beat 1 — the downbeat. Land here.
Beat 3 lit — mid-bar.

The large dot is beat 1 — it flashes white, slightly bigger than the others. This is your target moment. Everything you play in bars 2, 3 and 4 should be building toward resolving onto a chord tone as this dot fires again.

The bar counter to the right of the dots counts how many bars have passed. Useful for tracking phrase length — a good phrase is typically one or two bars long.

Fretboard

Reading the neck

The fretboard shows frets 1–13, with the nut on the left. String order matches standard notation: high e at the top, low E at the bottom.

Every dot shows its function label — R for root, 3 for third, 5 for fifth, and the note name for other scale tones. The amber root dots also have a faint glow ring so they stand out at a glance.

The fretboard updates live as the chord changes. Watch it shift when you use a multi-chord progression — the root moves, the 3rd moves, and suddenly you can see the relationship between chords on the neck rather than just hearing it.

The Show filter

The Show dropdown is a progressive focus control. Use it in order as you develop:

Root only
Only amber dots visible. One target per string position. Use this first — learn where every root note lives on the neck before you add anything else. One note landed correctly beats twelve notes played randomly.
Chord tones only
Root, 3rd, and 5th. Three possible landing notes per area of the neck. Once you can hit the root reliably, start choosing between these three. The 3rd on a landing note is your biggest vocabulary upgrade.
All pentatonic
Full pentatonic scale shown. Use this when chord tones feel automatic. The purple dots are now your passing tones — notes to move through, targeting the amber/blue/green on the way in and out of phrases.
Progressions

Chord progressions explained

Roman numerals refer to scale degrees — how far each chord root sits from the tonic. In A minor: i = Am, iv = Dm, VII = G, VI = F, III = C.

Progression In A minor What to practise
i (tonic only) Am → Am → Am → Am Landing. No chord changes — pure focus on targeting the root on beat 1. Start here.
i – VII – VI – VII Am – G – F – G The most common minor progression in pop and rock. Smooth root motion. Watch the fretboard shift on each change.
i – iv – VII – i Am – Dm – G – Am Blues-flavoured movement. The iv chord (Dm) is the tension point — its root lands on D, a new target each bar.
i – VI – III – VII Am – F – C – G Classic four-chord minor loop. More harmonic movement — harder to track, but great for developing chord tone awareness under pressure.
I – V – vi – IV (major) A – E – F#m – D Switch Mode to Major before using this. The I–V–vi–IV is behind hundreds of pop songs. The vi chord is minor — notice how the fretboard changes to show its minor 3rd.
Phrase tips

The six tips at the bottom

The six cards below the fretboard are not decorative — they are the complete phrasing curriculum in condensed form. Click any card to highlight it as a session focus.

Tip 1
Land on the root
On beat 1, aim for the amber dot. Even one right note sounds musical.
Tip 2
Three notes, one phrase
Pick root + one above + one below. Play that loop. Stop. Breathe. Repeat.
Tip 3
Silence is a note
After a phrase, rest for two beats. The gap makes what came before sound intentional.
Tip 4
Steal from singers
Climb to a peak note, then resolve down to the root. That contour is 90% of melody.
Tip 5
Repeat, then vary
Play a 3-note motif. Play it again. Change the last note the third time. That's development.
Tip 6
Follow the chord
When the chord changes, shift your target. The 3rd of the new chord resolves tension instantly.

Work through these in order across your first six sessions. One tip per session is enough. By session six, all six habits should be running simultaneously without conscious thought.

Practice plan

Six sessions, one skill each

Each session is 15–20 minutes. Settings are specific — do not improvise the settings until the session's one goal is solid.

Session 1 — Root targeting

Settings: Key A · Minor · Prog: i (tonic only) · BPM 65 · Show: Root only

Play single notes only. On every beat 1, land on an amber dot. Anywhere on the neck. Do not worry about what happens on beats 2, 3, 4 — that is travel time.

Done when: You hit amber on beat 1 consistently without thinking about it.

Session 2 — Three-note phrases

Settings: Key A · Minor · Prog: i · BPM 65 · Show: Root only

Pick three adjacent amber dots. Play them as a phrase — any order, any rhythm. End the phrase on the root. Then stop for two beats. Then repeat with a variation.

Done when: You can play and stop intentionally, not just run out of notes.

Session 3 — Add the 3rd and 5th

Settings: Key A · Minor · Prog: i · BPM 65 · Show: Chord tones only

Now amber, blue, and green dots are visible. Extend your phrases to include the 3rd (blue) or 5th (green), but always resolve back to amber at the end. Notice how ending on the 3rd sounds like a question; ending on the root sounds like an answer.

Done when: You can choose your landing note rather than just hoping for the best.

Session 4 — Open up the full scale

Settings: Key A · Minor · Prog: i · BPM 65 · Show: All pentatonic

Purple dots appear — the remaining pentatonic notes. Treat them as connecting tissue. A phrase can start or pass through purple, but should land on amber, blue, or green. Notice which purple notes feel tense and which feel neutral.

Done when: You're choosing targets, not just running the visible dots in order.

Session 5 — First chord change

Settings: Key A · Minor · Prog: i – VII – VI – VII · BPM 60 · Show: Chord tones only

Now the chord changes every bar. Watch the chord name in the display. As each new chord arrives, your target shifts. The fretboard updates to show you where. Slow right down — 60 BPM gives you time to look, locate, and decide.

Done when: You can follow at least two of the four chords before losing the thread.

Session 6 — Develop a motif

Settings: Key A · Minor · Prog: i – VII – VI – VII · BPM 70 · Show: All pentatonic

Play a three-note phrase. Play the same phrase again. On the third repeat, change just one note. That is musical development. BB King built entire solos from this pattern. Listen back and notice: the repetition makes the variation sound intentional, not random.

Done when: You can hear yourself developing an idea rather than just reacting.

Quick reference

At a glance

  • 01 Set Key and Mode to match any backing track you want to play over. Most YouTube blues tracks state the key in the title.
  • 02 Start with Show: Root only until you can land on amber on beat 1 without looking at the fretboard first.
  • 03 Keep BPM at 60–70 until you are deliberately choosing notes. Speed is earned, not set.
  • 04 Watch the chord name on multi-chord progressions, not just the fretboard. Knowing why the dots moved is what builds real fretboard knowledge.
  • 05 Use the phrase tips as session anchors. One tip active per session. Trying to apply all six at once applies none of them.
  • 06 Stop and listen. The most musical thing in any session is the silence you leave after a phrase. If you never stop, you're practising scales. If you stop, you're practising music.
