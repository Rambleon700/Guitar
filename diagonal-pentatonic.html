Diagonal Pentatonic

Box 1 and Box 2 of the minor pentatonic, fused into one ascending run that climbs the neck instead of staying parked in a single position.

What's a diagonal pentatonic?

Most players first learn the pentatonic scale as five separate "boxes" — fixed hand positions you stay inside, one fret-range at a time. A diagonal pentatonic breaks out of that habit: instead of resetting your hand for each box, you let the shape climb the neck as you cross from string to string. Same notes, same scale — just connected into one continuous run instead of five disconnected shapes sitting side by side.

How this shape works

This particular diagonal plays Box 1 on the bottom two strings, then shifts up into Box 2 territory for the remaining four — a smooth two-position run with no backtracking. Switch between the "Box 1" and "Box 2" buttons above to see each piece on its own, then hit "Diagonal" to watch them fuse into the glowing connector line. Notice the line only ever moves up and to the right — that's the diagonal.

Using it

Everything is anchored to the root note (the gold dots) — change the dropdown and the whole shape slides with it, since pentatonic shapes are movable up and down the neck regardless of key. Tap any dot, or hit "Play shape," to hear it. Practice it ascending string-by-string, then reverse it on the way down. Once it's under your fingers, try landing on the higher root (top-right gold dot) as a target tone — it's the same idea as the BB King box, just stretched further up the neck.
